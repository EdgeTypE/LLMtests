Biography

The Man Who Saw Tomorrow

Sir Arthur Charles Clarke (1917–2008) was a British science fiction writer, futurist, inventor, undersea explorer, and television host. He is best remembered for co-writing the screenplay for 2001: A Space Odyssey with Stanley Kubrick — widely regarded as the most influential science fiction film ever made.

In 1945 — more than a decade before the first satellite — Clarke published a technical paper proposing geostationary communications satellites. The orbit he described is now named the Clarke Orbit. He received numerous honors, including a knighthood from the British Empire and the Heinlein Award for lifetime achievement in science fiction.

Clarke spent much of his later life in Sri Lanka (formerly Ceylon), where he pursued his twin passions: the ocean and the stars. He explored the Great Barrier Reef, wrote extensively on the Indian Ocean, and hosted television series blending science, speculation, and wonder.

Clarke's Laws of Prediction

Distilled wisdom on the nature of progress, the boundaries of knowledge, and the threshold where engineering becomes wonder.

I
The Law of Possibility
“When a distinguished but elderly scientist states that something is possible, he is almost certainly right. When he states that something is impossible, he is very probably wrong.”

The Law of Magic

Timeline

Predictions & Milestones

Clarke's ideas often arrived decades before the technology caught up. Here are the moments when his vision became reality.

1945
The Communications Satellite
Publishes "Extra-Terrestrial Relays" in Wireless World, describing geostationary satellites for global communication. The concept becomes reality in 1964 with Syncom 3.
1964
Geostationary Orbit Realized
Syncom 3 becomes the first geostationary communications satellite, validating Clarke's 1945 proposal. The orbit is later officially designated the Clarke Orbit.
1968
2001: A Space Odyssey Premieres
Co-written with Stanley Kubrick, the film sets a new standard for scientific accuracy in cinema — from spinning space stations to Pan Am space planes and the HAL 9000 AI.
1979
The Space Elevator Vision
The Fountains of Paradise presents the first fully realized space elevator concept. Today, companies and space agencies actively research carbon-nanotube tethers for orbital elevators.
2001
HAL's Year Arrives
The year 2001 comes and goes. While HAL 9000 remains fiction, AI systems begin matching — and in some tasks surpassing — the capabilities Clarke imagined for his infamous computer.
